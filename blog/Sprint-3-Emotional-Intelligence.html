What does Emotional Intelligence mean?

EQ was first proposed by researchers Mayer and Salovey in 1990 as way in which the field of psychology could identify and research differences in individual capabilities related to emotion. This model of emotional intelligence led to the creation of tests that were designed top gauge the emotional abilities of subjects, and thus, EQ (Emotional Quotient) was born.

Emotional Intelligence theory has created a model of EQ whereby traditional, culturally valenced ideas about the nature of emotions are reconfigured. In this reconfigured schema, emotions are an aspect of the human intelligence matrix. They become useful sources of information regarding the endogenous reactions humans experience in response to exogenous stimuli. As Salovey and Mayer defined it (1990), emotional intelligence is:

“The ability to monitor one’s own and other’s feelings, to discriminate among them, and to use this information to guide thinking and action.”

This has been further refined into four branches of distinct yet related “branches” of emotional skills.

BRANCH 1.0

The first branch is the ability to perceive and decipher human emotions, not only those of others but one’s own too.

BRANCH 2.0

The second branch relates to the active use of emotions; moving beyond merely “feeling”, an individual can understand emotions (to a degree) and thereby apply them to constructive ends. The tail no longer wags the dog.

BRANCH 3.0

The third branch of emotional intelligence builds upon the second, this branch proposes the meta-skill of emotional fluency. This meansan individual can “speak” in the language of emotions and is capable of discerning small “grammatical” variables between emotional states e.g. happiness and ebullience.

BRANCH 4.0

The fourth branch deals with the management of emotions and encompasses the most complex set of abilities measured by EQ tests. The ability to manage emotions, in this case, refers not merely to the conscious regulation of one’s own emotions, but to that of other individuals as well.

IQ

Intelligence Quotient (IQ) is a score derived from standardized tests which have been designed to measure human intelligence. Although the concept of human intelligence has evolved over time and IQ tests have changed in various ways, the foundational concepts of IQ have stayed fairly constant.

IQ x EQ

Both IQ and EQ measure cognitive capabilities but focus upon two distinct regions of socially meaningful praxis. Whereas IQ may be an effective predictor of academic success or the ability to excel in fields which prize the understanding of highly technical and abstract concepts, EQ may be a better predictor of success in areas which require interpersonal skills and emotional labour. Individuals with high EQs are often successful in roles that require negotiation, mediation, collaboration, and that involve caring for or understanding the needs of others.

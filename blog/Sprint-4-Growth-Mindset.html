Linking the Flame

Dark Souls is a now (in)famous series of games, part of a wider catalogue, from the Japanese game development studio, FromSoftware. Its infamy/fame has arisen as a result of two strange and interlinked fundamental design concepts; the player must experience the game a set of pseudo-sisyphean struggles and at no point can they expect enlightenment through narrative dumping.For most people, when confronted with this type of experience, the natural reaction is one of exasperated frustration and, no doubt, the throwing of controller's across the room. Their mistake, and my own initially, is to incorrectly assume that the game developers are suggesting they are not good enough to play. When in fact, they are being told to persevere through repeated failure, learn, adapt and "get good". Dark Souls was in fact challenging players to adapt what has since become know as, Growth Mindset. Through a form of recursive learning which engages a process of cognitive growth via neural plasticity, alongside a healthy and positive relationship with challenge, the player not only becomes happy, like sisyphus, but also breaks free of their digital groundhog day.

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity refers to the brain's ability to reorganise itself by forming new neural connections throughout life. This allows neurons in the brain to compensate for TBI, brain disease as well as respond to novel challenges created by new exogenous stimuli e.g. learning to code!

Growth Mindset

Growth mindset is concept developed by psychologist Carol Dweck; it is a belief that abilities and intelligence can be developed through hard work. Individuals with a growth a mindset embrace challenge, persist in the face of defeat, view effort as a path to mastery, learn from criticism and find inspiration in the achievements of others.

Linking

Both concepts are mutually reinforcing, the understanding one can augment the application of the other. Going forward, as part of my mindfulness exercise, I will remember the central precepts of both neuroplasticity and growth mindset. Remembering that neuroplasticity proves that a new dog can in fact be taught new tricks enables me to be open to the idea that failure and difficulty doesn't mean the closing of another door, rather that they represent the possibilities of an open one.

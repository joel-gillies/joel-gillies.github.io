The Brick Wall I Ran Into

I got stuck quite a lot this week, especially during the JavaScript Kata. For the first two Kata, I felt like understood "the surroundings", and that I was dealing with known knowns and known unknowns... Probably a few unknown knowns too! But from the third Kata onwards I felt like I was in a foreign land and everyone was speaking Hungarian and the map was in Greek. That is, I was dealing with unknown knowns and unknown unknowns. The most enlightening moment was when I was writing a .split() function and then a .join() function. To me it seemed like my code should be working, but the tests were still coming back negative. I started testing it using P5 and realised my function and join methods weren't doing what I wanted them to. I had forgotten to place a '\_' within their brackets. This is the latest in a series of learning moments over the past few weeks where my attention to detail and reading comprehension has been found lacking. It's been really cool to realise that when put under new forms of stress and strain, my weak points become apparent and at the same time appear more fixable.

Elegance in Problem Solving

I am not sure if any of my coding could be considered elegant at this point, but I did quite enjoy using the a .find() method in the "whereAreYou" part of the 5th Kata. It really brought home to me the importance of taking the time to read instructions, read over the code at hand and think about what it is you are really being asked to do. Despite the relatively large amount of code within which Scooby was hiding, the resolution was relatively simple, short and sweet.

The Technique of Talking to (Rubber) Ducks

Pseudo-Code

I feel quite comfortable using pseudo code, and I think it is the technique I have had the most success with. Removing the problem presented from the coding syntax downward into its own domain and applying structured, but simple, English to it. It then becomes a plain language narrative where the code is restructured as a single construct with a beginning and end. The formality is introduced using a simple language control flow: While For If-Else etc. For myself, it makes coding far less about byzantine syntax (which at this stage is incredibly arcane to me) and more about problem solving. It also acts as a learning format, as translating actual code into pseudo code and back helps me understand syntax and semantics (bit by bit).

Quacks and Quackery

I was pretty keen (but also put off by the oddness of it) to try rubber ducking, and it did work quite well alongside pseudo coding. Speaking the problem out loud to myself forced me to be purposeful with my words, I couldn't simply stream of consciousness whatever came into my head. I tried that and it made no sense. Likewise, it helped me check my pseudo code once I had written it, when there was a logical error or aspect of the challenge I had misunderstood it became obvious.

Google

Google can be a mixed bag, especially when you are searching for information on concepts you yourself are barely familiar with. Many of the resources provided can themselves be a chore to understand. But having used it for years at university, I feel pretty comfortable with my ability to pose queries which return quality results (more often than not).

Console Logging and ERROR messages

I can definitely see the utility of console logging and error messages, I thought the Kata exercise was really excellent at illustrating this. Unfortunately I do not use Console Logging nearly enough, because I don;t yet feel very comfortable with it. But going forward this will be something I force myself to do at every opportunity. The error messages, thought sometimes still a bit cryptic, were a great signpost to issues that otherwise may have remained somewhat opaque.

Asking for Help

I am terrible at asking for help within structured learning environments, no doubt a hold over from being the dunce of Highschool Mathematics! When you post a question in a discord server, there is no way to keep you supposedly embarrassing ignorance between yourself and your tutor. This will be something that just takes practice, the more you ask, the less foolish you feel. Questions may be indeed be stupid, but not asking them is infinitely more so.
